# Location proof evaluation

When a [location proof](/concepts/location-proof-structure) is submitted for verification, Astral evaluates it through a three-phase process. The output is a **credibility vector** — a structured assessment across multiple dimensions, not a binary yes/no.

## The three evaluation phases

### Phase 1: Stamp checks

Each [location stamp](/concepts/location-stamps) is evaluated independently for internal validity:

- **Signatures** — Are the cryptographic signatures valid? Do they chain to a trusted source for this [proof-of-location system](/concepts/pol-systems)?
- **Structure** — Does the location stamp conform to the expected format for its [plugin](/plugins/overview)?
- **Signal consistency** — Are the raw signals internally consistent? For example, do GPS coordinates, Wi-Fi access point readings, and cellular tower measurements within a ProofMode location stamp agree with each other?

A location stamp that fails these checks provides no useful evidence — its results are reported but won't contribute positively to the overall credibility.

### Phase 2: Correlation checks (multi-stamp location proofs)

For location proofs with multiple location stamps from independent proof-of-location systems, the evaluator cross-correlates the evidence:

- **Independence** — Are the location stamps from genuinely independent sources? Two location stamps from the same proof-of-location system or the same device aren't independent. Independence is assessed based on plugin type, device identity, and trust model.
- **Agreement** — Do the independent location stamps agree on location and timing? Evidence from unrelated sources that converges on the same location is substantially more convincing than any single source.

This phase is what makes multi-factor location proofs powerful. Agreement between independent sources is hard to forge because an attacker would need to compromise multiple unrelated systems simultaneously.

### Phase 3: Claim assessment

The evaluator compares the observed evidence (from the location stamps) against the asserted claim:

- **Spatial consistency** — Does the observed location from the location stamps fall within the claimed location and radius?
- **Temporal consistency** — Does the temporal footprint of the evidence overlap with the claimed time range?
- **Overall support** — Given the strength of the evidence (phases 1 and 2), how well does it support this specific claim?

## The credibility vector

The output of evaluation is a credibility vector with four dimensions:

| Dimension | What it measures | Range |
|-----------|-----------------|-------|
| **Spatial** | How well the observed locations support the claimed location | 0–1 |
| **Temporal** | How well the observation times support the claimed time range | 0–1 |
| **Validity** | Internal validity of the location stamps (signatures, structure, signals) | 0–1 |
| **Independence** | How independent and corroborating the evidence sources are | 0–1 |

An overall **confidence** score (0–1) summarizes across dimensions, but the per-dimension scores are often more useful — they tell you *where* the evidence is strong or weak.

## Confidence is not probability

A confidence score of 0.8 does not mean "80% probability the claim is true." It means "the evidence is reasonably strong based on the available information." Calibrating confidence scores to true probability is an open research problem.

What the confidence score does express: the strength of the evidence relative to what the proof-of-location systems can provide. A 0.8 from a single device attestation reflects different underlying assurance than a 0.8 from three independent proof-of-location systems — which is why the per-dimension breakdown and individual location stamp results matter.

## Application-level decisions

Astral evaluates and reports. Applications decide.

The credibility vector gives applications enough information to make risk-appropriate decisions. A social check-in app might accept a confidence of 0.5. A compliance system might require 0.9 with at least two independent sources. A land title registry might require the maximum available assurance.

The threshold is always the application's choice — Astral does not impose minimum confidence requirements.
